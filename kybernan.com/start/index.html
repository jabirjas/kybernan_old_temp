Getting started

Start with a framework, begin by implementing one basic change, and then iterate and continuously improve. Move from a partial understanding and a limited scope to eventually becoming consciously proficient across a broad scope and ultimately executing masterfully beyond the scope and without the need for conscious attention.

The framework is simple. To operate at higher levels of productivity, one must improve the tools they use, the skills and abilities they have, the process flow they follow, and the leverage they apply.

Card

The four basic tools, that this quick start guide will touch on, are: the card, the calendar, the clock, and the notebook. Each has a purpose. All four should not be implemented at the same time nor to the same degree. It does not matter if they are implemented digitally or with paper, but when each is implemented, each should be implemented in such a way that each tool is within reach at nearly every minute of the day.

The starting place is with the card. Simply write down the six non-negotiable outcomes that will be accomplished during the day in rank order (the most important being first). There are far more sophisticated ways of using this card, but this is the first building block. While not required, it is highly recommended that using a small index card (3×5 or 4×6) is a great way to start building the habit of using checklists. Each card can go a long way to develop focus, build efficient and stress-free processes, ingrain the habit of execution, and accelerate planning and decision making.

Calendar

The calendar is the next step. The basic value of the calendar is to ensure one does not over-allocate one’s time. To begin using a calendar, write into the calendar every commitment made at the time you expect to meet the commitment. Too often the essence of a calendar is defined as a tool to remind us of appointments or as a tool to coordinate group activities, it certainly can do those things, but until a calendar is seen primarily as an allocation tool for meeting commitments that take time to fulfill, it lacks the power to transform our productivity.

In addition to being used for a variety of reasons that are not mutually exclusive, there are more advanced ways of using calendars than simply best-guess scheduling of commitments. These include using overlays, allocation targets, summary reporting, ticklers, reverse planning, and so forth. However, the first and most fundamental practice for using a calendar is to support keeping commitments.

Clock

While a clock is obviously used to help someone understand their relationship to their calendar, more fundamentally a clock is a cueing tool – when to start and when to stop. The basic use case for a clock when initially becoming more productive, is as a cueing tool to stop working. Bracketing work (limiting focus to a single task for some number of minutes) improves productivity significantly. It forces the mind to focus, bounds parkinson’s law, and provides for a rest interval.

As clock management proficiency improves, there are more techniques that can be applied, most notably the Pomodoro Technique developed by Francesco Cirillo — read more here and here.

Book

Capturing data, refining concepts, and reviewing notes for memorization and deeper understanding of events are just a few of the wonderful things one can do with a notebook — all of which are key to becoming more productive.

But a new habit must be cultivated: note-taking.

There are a wide range of note-taking techniques and those who are truly productive are proficient in multiple techniques and know when to apply which, whether its mind-mapping, charting, outlining, Carnegie method, and so forth. There are also a wide range of benefits that come from continuous note-taking that go beyond simply recalling a string of numbers or random quotes. An effective note-taking and notebook management can support drafting a personal constitution, managing projects effectively, leaving behind a documented legacy, and building a first-class knowledge management system.
